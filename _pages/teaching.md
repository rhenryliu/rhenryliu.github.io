In addition to my research, I also really enjoy teaching. I think great teaching is an integral part of being a good researcher. I try to make courses I teach as engaging and useful as possible, and I always welcome feedback from students on how to improve the learning experience. If you are a past student with suggestions for me or for the courses I've taught, feel free to email me.

I've taught a number of courses at UC Berkeley, most notably [Physics 188/288](https://classes.berkeley.edu/content/2025-fall-physics-188-001-lec-001) (Bayesian Data Analysis and Machine Learning for Physical Sciences), which I've helped develop and taught for three semesters ([Fall 2023](https://classes.berkeley.edu/content/2023-fall-physics-188-101-dis-101), [Fall 2024](https://classes.berkeley.edu/content/2024-fall-physics-188-101-dis-101), and [Fall 2025](https://classes.berkeley.edu/content/2025-fall-physics-188-101-dis-101)). The course introduces students to data analysis and machine learning and their central role in the physical sciences, spanning linear and nonlinear regression, supervised and unsupervised learning, Bayesian statistics and MCMC, and numerical methods such as optimization and Fourier analysis. I received the [Outstanding Graduate Student Instructor Award](https://gsi.berkeley.edu/programs-services/award-programs/ogsi/) for my work as a GSI in the course. 
I've also taught Physics 7A (Physics for Scientists and Engineers) and Physics 8A (Introductory Physics) in my early years of grad school.


In my time teaching Physics 188/288, I've written many of the course's discussion notes and developed a number of its coding assignments and all of its projects. Some of these materials are available below. Note that I may take them offline during the semester when the course is being taught, so please check back later if you don't see what you're looking for.
